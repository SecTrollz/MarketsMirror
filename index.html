The Algorithm

Eight specialist trees. Each reads one dimension of you.

MarketsMirror uses Random Forest — the same algorithm NASA uses on Mars to classify terrain, and the same framework Facebook uses to classify you. These 8 psycholinguistic dimensions are validated in 143,000+ academic studies. They're also what gets you segmented, targeted, and sold.

T1 · LIWC-22
First Person Density
Rate of "I" in your language. High = internalized emotional pain. Low = dissociation or suppressed self-concept. Pennebaker (1993).
→ Platform use: internalizing personality targeting
T2 · LIWC-22
Temporal Orientation
Past vs. future tense across the full session. Past-dominant = unresolved attachment. Future-dominant = anxiety, identity instability.
→ Platform use: retargeting pixel exploitation
T3 · LIWC-22
Negation Architecture
What you say you are NOT reveals the disowned self. The honest confession lives in the clause after "but." Weighted 2× by the parser.
→ Platform use: emotional vulnerability window detection
T4 · LIWC-22
Relational Grammar
"I was left" vs. "I left." Who holds agency in your story of being hurt is a primary attachment wound signal used in behavioral segmentation.
→ Platform use: relationship anxiety audience targeting
T5 · LIWC-22
Valence Collision
Positive and negative emotion words in the same sentence = chronic ambivalence. The thing you cannot close is the thing the algorithm keeps open.
→ Platform use: engagement amplification via unresolved tension
T6 · LIWC-22
Avoidance Topology
Topics circled but not landed on. Sentence pivots after disclosure. The shape of what is not said is the shape of the wound — and the targeting category.
→ Platform use: gradual-trust content sequencing
T7 · LIWC-22
Rumination Index
Density of cognitive processing words: because, think, realize, wonder. High = analytically inside the wound. This is the "insight-framed content" targeting signal.
→ Platform use: analytical segment, self-help ad delivery
T8 · LIWC-22
Clout / Authenticity
Performed authority vs. private emotional state. The gap between public and private self is measurable and commercially valuable. Kacewicz et al. (2014).
→ Platform use: social status anxiety targeting

This isn't just about ads. It's about what democracy requires to function.

A functioning democracy depends on an informed electorate making decisions based on their actual values and real information. The behavioral data economy is built on the opposite of that. Platforms don't make money when you're informed and decisive. They make money when you're engaged — which means uncertain, reactive, and emotionally activated.

Cambridge Analytica operationalized psychographic scoring — the exact framework this tool demonstrates — to micro-target voters in multiple national elections using Facebook data collected without consent. The model didn't need your vote. It needed your psychological profile to deliver the right emotional trigger at the right moment to change it.

The checkbox you didn't read when you made your account? It wasn't designed to inform you. It was designed to appear to inform you while obscuring what you were actually agreeing to. Every lawyer who reviewed those terms of service knows exactly what informed consent looks like. What got deployed was something else.

When companies have a business model where everything is free and they still generate trillion-dollar revenue — you are the product. Not the customer. Not the user. The inventory.

The Shrug Response
"They're tracking us anyway. Everyone knows. So what?"

Say that out loud and notice how it feels. It feels like clarity — like you've thought it through and landed somewhere reasonable. You haven't. You've landed exactly where the system needs you.

Researchers call it the privacy paradox: most people express concern about tracking and change nothing. This gap is not a personal failure. It is a measurable, modeled outcome that platforms engineer through interface design, notification timing, and algorithmic content selection. A user who shrugs and scrolls generates data indefinitely. That is the most profitable kind of user.

There is also something more specific happening. Recommendation algorithms are optimized for engagement, not for your cognitive clarity. If outrage, anxiety, or emotional volatility keeps you scrolling, the system amplifies those states — because they correlate with ad impressions. Over time, this degrades sustained attention and reduces the tolerance for complexity required to act on information like this. The feedback loop maintains itself by making everything feel too big to address.

The shrug is worth billions of dollars to the people who taught it to you. These four tools take less than ten minutes. They cost nothing. The only requirement is deciding that the version of you who used to give a damn about things is still in there somewhere — because they are, and they just read all the way to this sentence.
